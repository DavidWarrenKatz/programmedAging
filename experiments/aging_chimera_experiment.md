# Experiment: Generating a Hybrid Organism with Half the Cells from a Short-lived and Half from a Long-lived Species


Based on this paper
- Jorge de la Rosa, María A. Freije, Francisco G. Osorio, et al., and Carlos López-Otín. *Prelamin A causes progeria through cell-extrinsic mechanisms and prevents cancer invasion*. Nature Communications, 2013. [Link to paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms3268)

## Purpose
This experiment aims to explore the contribution of cellular environment versus genetic programming in determining lifespan. By creating an organism composed of cells from both a short-lived and a long-lived species, we aim to test how the aging process is influenced by the cellular environment, genetics, and potentially by intercellular signaling between different cell types.

## Experimental Design
### Species Selection
- **Species A (Short-lived)**: A species that has a naturally short lifespan, such as the *killifish* (*Nothobranchius furzeri*), which has a lifespan of around 4-6 months.
- **Species B (Long-lived)**: A closely related species with a longer lifespan, such as another killifish species (*Nothobranchius kadleci*), which can live up to 1-2 years.

### Procedure
1. **Embryonic Chimerism**: Using embryonic stem cells from both species, generate a chimera organism that has approximately 50% of its cells from Species A (short-lived) and 50% from Species B (long-lived).
2. **Cell Type Distribution**: Ensure that the cells from both species are evenly distributed throughout the organism, including both germline and somatic cells.
3. **X-Chromosome Inactivation Analogy**: Similar to the experiment using X-chromosome inactivation in progeroid mice (where cells were randomly inactivated to simulate a progeroid condition in only half the organism), the chimeric organism would have half of its cells genetically predisposed to short lifespan and the other half to long lifespan.

## Hypotheses and Predictions

### Outcome 1: **Short Lifespan**
- **Prediction**: The organism could exhibit a lifespan close to that of the short-lived species (Species A).
- **Interpretation**: This result would suggest that the aging process is primarily driven by the "weakest link" hypothesis, where cells from the short-lived species trigger systemic aging or dysfunction, overwhelming any potential longevity signals from the long-lived species.

### Outcome 2: **Long Lifespan**
- **Prediction**: The organism could exhibit a lifespan closer to that of the long-lived species (Species B).
- **Interpretation**: This outcome would imply that the environment or systemic factors provided by the long-lived species' cells can provide a protective or rejuvenating effect to the short-lived cells. It may support the idea that longevity is influenced by intercellular signaling or systemic regulation rather than intrinsic cellular aging.

### Outcome 3: **Intermediate Lifespan**
- **Prediction**: The organism could show a lifespan that is intermediate between Species A and Species B.
- **Interpretation**: This result would suggest that the aging process is a complex interaction between cellular programming and systemic factors, with no one type of cell dominating the aging process. The organism would essentially "average" the aging rates of both species.

### Outcome 4: **Tissue-specific Aging**
- **Prediction**: Different tissues could age at different rates, with tissues predominantly composed of cells from Species A (short-lived) aging faster, and tissues predominantly composed of Species B (long-lived) aging slower.
- **Interpretation**: This would imply that aging is partially a local phenomenon, with individual cells or tissues driving their own aging, independent of systemic signals. This outcome would highlight the importance of cell-autonomous aging processes.

## Controls
- **Chimera with Only Species A Cells**: To confirm that the experimental manipulation does not alter the lifespan of the short-lived species.
- **Chimera with Only Species B Cells**: To confirm that the experimental manipulation does not alter the lifespan of the long-lived species.

## Implications of the Study
This experiment could provide insights into the interplay between genetic programming and environmental factors in determining lifespan. Understanding whether systemic factors can override genetic predispositions for aging could have significant implications for developing therapies to extend lifespan or treat age-related diseases.
