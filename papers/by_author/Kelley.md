# David R. Kelley

David R. Kelley is a researcher focusing on the molecular and cellular mechanisms underlying aging and muscle differentiation. His work explores how aging reveals latent features in muscle stem cells and identifies an energy barrier in myogenesis in murine models.

### 2. [**Differentiation Reveals Latent Features of Aging and an Energy Barrier in Murine Myogenesis**](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2211124721003624?via%3Dihub)
**Jacob C. Kimmel et al. and David R. Kelley, Cell Reports, 2021**

**Summary**:  
This study investigates the molecular changes in muscle stem cells (MuSCs) that occur with aging, focusing on how these cells differentiate and the energy requirements for this process. By profiling MuSCs from young and aged mice, Kelley and colleagues uncovered aging-associated transcriptional changes that highlight an energy barrier in the differentiation process. They identified specific gene expression patterns in aged MuSCs that inhibit effective differentiation, suggesting potential molecular targets to restore myogenic function in aged muscles.

**Identifying Aging-Associated Molecular Changes in Muscle Stem Cells**:  
The team performed single-cell RNA sequencing on MuSCs isolated from young (3 months) and aged (24 months) mice. This analysis revealed that aging induces transcriptional reprogramming in MuSCs, marked by upregulation of pathways involved in inflammatory responses and downregulation of genes essential for muscle differentiation. A latent feature of aging observed in the study was a shift in metabolic gene expression that suggested an increased energy requirement for aged MuSCs to undergo myogenesis.

**Energy Barrier in Myogenesis**:  
Through metabolic assays and functional validation, the authors identified an "energy barrier" to myogenesis in aged MuSCs. This barrier is defined by a higher ATP demand during differentiation in aged cells. Metabolic interventions that increase ATP availability were shown to partially restore differentiation capacity in aged MuSCs, highlighting the role of energy metabolism in maintaining muscle regenerative potential during aging.

**Aging-Associated Features and Potential Therapeutic Targets**:  
By pinpointing the metabolic and transcriptional disruptions in aged MuSCs, Kelley and colleagues propose that targeting specific pathways to lower the energy demand in aged muscle cells may help rejuvenate their regenerative capacity. Key pathways identified include mTOR signaling and mitochondrial biogenesis, both of which contribute to the elevated ATP demand observed in aged MuSCs.

**Commentary**:  
This research provides new insights into the molecular mechanisms of aging in muscle tissue and underscores the role of energy metabolism in muscle stem cell function. The findings suggest that therapies aimed at modulating metabolic pathways may be beneficial in countering muscle degeneration associated with aging. Targeting the identified energy barrier could improve myogenic differentiation and muscle health in elderly populations.
